I Dreamed You First: A Love That Found Me
Chapter 1: A Heart Full of Maybes
How is it going to be?
New state. New people. New friends. New me.
How is it actually going to be?
Will it be exciting? Or will this place change me in ways I don’t expect? Will I want to run back home in a day? Or will this place—this air, this water, this everything—make me forget my tiny, sweet little well that I come from… and show me a version of life I had only imagined from far away?
These were the exact thoughts running through the mind of a seventeen-year-old girl who had just stepped out of home for the first time—now sitting alone on a quiet bench after her introductory seminar, on the very first day of her bachelor’s degree, where everything around her felt new… beautifully unknown.
Hi, I am Naina… and this is my story.
I come from the kind of place where everybody knows everybody… and somehow, everybody knows your business too.
Raised by simple, grounded parents, studying in a small neighbourhood school surrounded by the same close-knit gang, with that slow, simple, slightly chaotic suburban vibe stitched into everything.
If we needed milk, we didn’t “go shopping.” We ran to the dairy farm. Eggs? Straight to the poultry for the freshest ones—not something picked off a supermarket shelf.
Life was close, comforting, and simple in a way you don’t fully appreciate until you leave it.
My dad, being a government employee, was always on the move—transfer after transfer, new places every few years. But somehow, me, my mom, and my elder sister Nithya stayed rooted in my town, holding on to one place for the sake of our education.
So while his world kept changing, ours stayed the same—steady, predictable… and quietly rooted.
Growing up in a place like that, I guess it’s easy to imagine what my one constant, quietly loud dream was—to step beyond the tiny little world I had always known and see what else life had waiting for me.
It wasn’t always dramatic… just a soft, steady thought that followed me everywhere. Every morning, every routine day… there’s more out there… go find it.
But then came the bigger question… how?
It’s not like I could just go to my dad and ask for money to chase that dream—he doesn’t exactly have a Swiss bank account hidden somewhere.
And slowly… something started to make sense.
If I wanted to leave, I had to earn my way out.
Education became my ticket out—my first real plan for freedom.
A good rank, a scholarship, a reason strong enough to step out—that’s what I worked for. And through all the late nights, quiet stress, and holding on to that tiny dream… somehow, it actually worked.
And now I’m here.
At a university I had once only seen in brochures and YouTube vlogs.
With a 50% scholarship.
Sitting on this bench.
Right in the middle of the life I used to imagine.
The rest of the day unfolded quietly, almost as if it were trying not to overwhelm me any further.
I returned to my room and began arranging my belongings—each movement slow, deliberate, like I was trying to make this place feel a little more mine. The room was small, meant for four of us, with just enough space to fit four beds, four desks, and narrow almirahs that somehow had to hold pieces of our lives within them.
I chose the bed closest to the door. It felt like the only spot that could offer a little air, a little movement—something that made me feel like I wasn’t completely closed in.
My desk, though, I arranged with care. Books stacked neatly, stationery placed in order. It wasn’t much, but it felt like claiming a corner of this new world as my own.
My roommates were… somewhere in between everything. Not warm enough to feel like comfort, not distant enough to feel unkind. We spoke in brief sentences, polite smiles passing between us, all of us careful, all of us adjusting in our own ways.
Earlier that day, while leaving me at the hostel, my father had introduced me to a girl—Lahari, his colleague’s daughter—and her friend. At the time, it had seemed reassuring, like a small thread of connection in a place where I knew no one.
By evening, I wasn’t so sure.
They had invited me over for dinner that night.
And saying no didn’t really feel like an option—not to unfamiliar kindness, not on my very first day. So I agreed, even though a part of me wasn’t ready yet, wasn’t sure of anything yet.
Maybe they’d become part of my story. Or maybe they’d just become another lesson.
Either way, it was only my first day—and already, it felt like something had quietly begun… something I didn’t understand yet.
I went to bed that night with all these thoughts running through my mind, not knowing which of them would fade away… and which of them would quietly change everything.
I woke up the next morning with a splitting headache and a head full of maybes.
This place had a talent for being loud before the sun was even properly awake.
The hallway was chaos. Absolute chaos. Doors banging, girls shouting, buckets scraping across the floor. The common bathroom at the end of the corridor had basically turned into a war zone. Every single girl in the hostel seemed to be standing there—waiting, arguing, negotiating—for a chance to shower.
And standing there in the middle of all that chaos, I had one very serious thought.
Being a boy suddenly seemed infinitely easier.
Because would I even care about a shower if I were one? No chance. I’d just spray some random amber body spray, throw on a Chinese-collar T-shirt that had been lying in the laundry for a week, and walk out like I owned the place.
No skincare. No hair drama. No emotional attachment to shampoo. Just vibes.
Meanwhile, I was seventeen, sleep-deprived, slightly dramatic—unfortunately, very dramatic—and already realising hostel life had its own personality.
Standing in a noisy hallway, with a head full of maybes… and absolutely no idea that my life was about to change.
Thankfully, my roommate had managed to save me a spot in the shower queue, so I somehow survived the morning chaos, got ready in record time, and headed downstairs for my first hostel breakfast.
The mess hall, ironically, was an actual mess.
The breakfast wasn’t terrible, surprisingly. They were serving dosas with two different chutneys and a very thick sambar.
Now listen—I know this is Tamil Nadu, and people here take their sambar very seriously… but why were the chutneys so watery? Chutney is supposed to sit on the dosa, not run across the plate like it’s trying to escape.
At that point, I had already started silently judging the hostel kitchen staff and questioning multiple life choices.
I somehow managed to force that breakfast down and started walking toward the Computer Science department—about a twenty-minute walk through the campus, which wasn’t too bad.
The path was unexpectedly beautiful. So much greenery everywhere. Tall trees, random little corners filled with students, morning sunlight slipping through the leaves like something straight out of one of those unrealistic college movies.
On the way, I passed the campus canteen. It didn’t look very impressive from the outside, but the smell?
Suspiciously convincing.
Of course, I was going to come back there later.
Or should I just bunk a class on the very first day and immediately establish myself as a mildly problematic girl with excellent priorities?
Tempting. Very tempting.
And somewhere between that chaotic energy, an overexcited heart, and absolutely no idea that this campus—and one person inside it—would quietly shape the rest of my life…
I walked into the department.
The Computer Science department building wasn’t exactly impressive. It was just a huge concrete structure with wide steps running through the middle—the kind you only see in dramatic movie mansions for absolutely no reason. The building was split into two wings, with classrooms stretching along both sides.
The ground floor had a few classrooms for the juniors, along with the Head of Department’s office and the staff room, where every professor had their own tiny cabin offices.
And somehow, that entire area radiated one very clear warning:
This is not a place you casually want to get called into.
The previous evening, the university had mailed us saying we had to collect our ID cards before attending classes because, apparently, attendance without an ID card was some sort of national crime here.
Which meant I had no choice.
I had to walk into the staff room.
Fantastic.
Well then…
Here I come, staff room.
Try not to be too intimidated by me.
To my surprise, the staff room was almost empty that early in the morning. Only one man was sitting there—probably the person in charge of handing out ID cards, mark sheets, and keeping records of everything important enough to ruin a student’s peace.
He actually seemed sweet, though. Very polite.
The only problem?
We were in Tamil Nadu… and suddenly my English-speaking skills felt completely useless.
He smiled at me warmly and said something in Tamil.
I had absolutely no idea what he said, but I smiled back anyway, like we were having a perfectly successful conversation.
Then came the repeated:
“Enna venum?”
At that moment, I didn’t know it meant, “What do you want?”
So there we were—him speaking rapid Tamil, me blinking respectfully, both of us deeply confused but committed to the interaction.
Somewhere between his “enna venum?”s and my “onnum puriyila…”s, I finally managed to explain that I was there for my ID card.
Mission successful.
Card collected, dignity slightly damaged, I sprinted out of the staff room and toward class because it was already five past nine… and apparently, I was officially late on the very first proper day of college.
Luckily, the classroom wasn’t too far away—and thankfully, it was on the ground floor.
By the time I reached, though, the class was already full.
And excuse me… what was this betrayal?
The last bench was occupied? Completely?
No. Absolutely not.
I had a reputation to maintain. I’d been a loyal last bencher my entire life, and I wasn’t about to let a new college ruin years of carefully built academic irresponsibility.
But apparently, everyone in this class had the exact same personality. Every single person had rushed straight to the back rows like it was some kind of survival instinct.
Except, of course, the front-row academic warriors—already sitting there with sharpened pencils, perfectly ruled notebooks, and the kind of dedication that probably made professors emotional.
You could just tell they were the type to score full marks, gatekeep notes like national treasure, and look personally offended if someone spoke during class.
With no other option, I finally found a seat in the third row from the back, tucked away in the left corner beside Amrutha and Saranya—two familiar faces I had met during the orientation the previous day.
The first day of class—with no lecturer in sight yet—gave me enough freedom to pull out my phone and call my mom for a quick little campus update.
I casually walked toward the window, turning the camera around to show her the classroom, the benches, the crowd, and the very glamorous beginning of my engineering life. After a few minutes, I told her I’d call later because it finally looked like class was about to start.
Or so I thought.
The moment I went back to my seat, a few guys who had been quietly sitting among us suddenly got up from the benches and walked toward the dais like actors revealing themselves halfway through a low-budget plot twist.
Seniors. Of course.
Apparently, they had blended in with the juniors just for dramatic effect, which felt extremely on-brand for people whose entire personality depended on making first years nervous.
They grabbed the mic with the confidence of celebrities arriving at an award show—loud voices, overdone attitude, gym-built egos, and approximately three functioning brain cells distributed equally among the group.
And apparently, they were there to “welcome” us.
How generous of them.
They started giving us a long, unnecessary speech about how the college worked—the departments, the rules, the facilities, the “culture” of the university—talking like they personally co-founded the institution with the dean.
The confidence was almost admirable. Especially for people who were probably struggling to pass half their internal exams themselves.
And just when I thought the performance was over, one of them suddenly pointed out that “some juniors already seem too hyper” because, apparently, I had been using my phone earlier.
Excuse me?
There wasn’t even a lecturer in class yet.
You were literally sitting among us pretending to be juniors five minutes ago like failed undercover agents. If anyone was invading somebody else’s space here, it was definitely not me.
It was my phone. My classroom. My first day.
Please go develop a personality outside of mic announcements and fake authority.
That part definitely got on my nerves.
But somehow, despite all of that… the rest of the day turned into something strangely unforgettable.
And trust me—we’re just getting to the good part.
After that little senior-performance episode, the class finally got a few peaceful minutes to recover from the collective embarrassment.
And then came another group of seniors.
Fantastic.
Because apparently, this university believed that suffering should arrive in instalments.
At that point, I was fully prepared for another long lecture about discipline, attendance, or some deeply inspiring rule about putting badminton rackets back in the correct place after playing shuttle.
But for once… this group was actually nice.
They were polite, normal, and spoke to us like actual human beings instead of prison wardens managing fresh inmates. They genuinely wanted us to feel comfortable, get familiar with the college, and interact with each other.
Strangely enough?
I liked them immediately.
They made all of us split into groups of four for some game—something between dumb charades, introductions, and complete chaos. It was actually fun.
Within minutes, people started shifting across benches, calling their friends over, and forming little groups around the classroom.
And then I noticed something life-changing.
Boys and girls were casually sitting together.
Like… normally. Freely. Nobody was acting like it was a criminal offence.
I genuinely had a small internal crisis.
Whattttttttttt?????
So this is how the outside world works?
Back home, even making eye contact with a boy for longer than three seconds would probably become breaking news by evening.
So naturally, I immediately decided I was absolutely sitting with guys.
I had already spent enough of my life listening to conversations about shampoos, earrings, and which actress had the best hair.
I was ready for some chaotic male energy and completely unnecessary confidence.
I spotted a space a little to my left where a bunch of North Indian guys were already sitting together, looking deeply invested in whatever nonsense they were discussing.
To me, it felt like the perfect opportunity to step out of my comfort zone and just go with the flow for once.
They didn’t seem intimidating. Just very… nerdy in a strangely confident way.
My only concern was figuring out what language I was supposed to approach them in.
But thankfully, North Indians usually survive pretty well with English—unlike the emotional survival challenge I had just experienced in the staff room earlier that morning.
Then the dumb charades officially began—and that’s when I realised just how aggressively dumb the game could actually become when engineering students were involved.
Every five minutes, someone invented a new rule nobody understood. Half the class acted like international-level performers while the other half looked like they were physically fighting invisible ghosts.
I couldn’t stop laughing.
The classroom slowly started loosening up. People were laughing loudly, screaming random movie names, accusing each other of cheating, and acting with the confidence of people who clearly had no future in acting.
Once everyone collectively got bored of dumb charades, the senior gang decided it was time to discover the “hidden talents” of the class.
Suddenly, people were singing, dancing, beatboxing, and performing skills nobody had asked for, but everybody was somehow encouraging anyway.
One of the girls I had just become friends with during the activity wanted to dance and asked me to play a song from my phone.
So there I was, proudly pulling out my precious Redmi Note 4—my birthday gift from my dad and basically the love of my life at that point.
And naturally, because the universe enjoys embarrassing people at very specific moments, that was the exact second my phone decided to malfunction.
I kept tapping the screen, trying to play the song like a desperate DJ at a failed college fest… but no sound came out.
Nothing.
Absolute betrayal.
And then came a voice from behind me.
Soft. Calm. Slightly shy… but confident in that effortless kind of way.
“Do you want me to take a look? Sometimes these speakers act weird.”
I turned around for a second—and genuinely forgot about the phone.
That was the first time I saw him.
And for a second, everything around me seemed to fade into the background.
Technically, he was from the North. But not entirely. He was Tamilian, brought up there, carrying this effortless blend of both worlds that made him stand out without even trying. There was something unusually comforting about him from the very beginning.
He was wearing a black-and-red checked shirt with faded blue jeans. Lean, slightly broad-shouldered already, with the beginning of a moustache that looked determined to grow into something serious.
And annoyingly enough… he was exactly the kind of boy I would fall for.
Not in a dramatic, movie-star way.
Just quietly attractive in the sort of way that sneaks up on you.
I suddenly became painfully aware of myself. The state of my hair. My expressions. The fact that my phone had decided to embarrass me at the worst possible moment.
Then he smiled.
And that smile completely disarmed me.
Soft. Effortless. The kind that lingers in your head longer than it should.
I handed him my phone, trying to hold myself together while he checked the speakers. Meanwhile, my mind had already stopped cooperating with me.
Part of me was worried he’d accidentally open some humiliating photo and destroy whatever little composure I had left on the very first day itself.
The other part of me was far too busy noticing him.
So there I was—head lowered, pretending to pay attention to the phone, while secretly looking at him every chance I got.
Apparently, my phone had been on silent the entire time, and I had been too distracted to even notice. He figured it out almost immediately, pressed a couple of buttons, and handed it back to me with the calm confidence of someone who just naturally knew what he was doing.
But somehow, that tiny interaction didn’t feel enough anymore.
Because now I wanted to know things.
Who was he?
Why did he suddenly feel so interesting?
And why had my attention shifted toward him this quickly?
It was ridiculous.
I didn’t even know him.
And yet, some quiet part of me already wanted the moment to last a little longer.
But instead, I just took the phone back from his hands, smiled like I was completely unaffected by the entire situation, and went back to the game, pretending nothing had happened.
